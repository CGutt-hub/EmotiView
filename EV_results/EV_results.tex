\subsection{Participant Characteristics and Data Quality}

Five participants (3 females, 2 males; mean age = 24.2 years, SD = 2.4, range = 21-27) completed the full experimental protocol. All participants were right-handed (Edinburgh Handedness Inventory laterality quotient > +40), native German speakers, and reported no history of neurological or psychiatric disorders. Data quality assessment revealed successful multimodal recording across all participants, with LSL synchronization maintaining temporal alignment across \gls{fNIRS}, \gls{EEG}, \gls{ECG}, and \gls{EDA} streams. Automated pipeline processing completed successfully for all five participants, generating 11 terminal output modules per participant (questionnaire assessments, physiological metrics, and neurophysiological measures). Individual participant results are presented in Figures \ref{fig:EV_002} through \ref{fig:EV_008}, with detailed metrics embedded in the PDF/A-compliant output documents.

\subsection{Subjective Affective Responses}

\subsubsection{Baseline Trait Measures}

Baseline questionnaires assessed trait motivational tendencies and pre-experimental affective state. \gls{BIS}/\gls{BAS} scores (\autoref{fig:bisbas}) revealed individual differences in behavioral inhibition and activation systems. Mean \gls{BIS} score across participants was M = 18.4 (SD = 3.2), indicating moderate sensitivity to punishment cues. \gls{BAS} subscales showed: Drive M = 10.2 (SD = 2.1), Fun-Seeking M = 11.6 (SD = 1.8), and Reward Responsiveness M = 16.8 (SD = 2.4). These values fall within normative ranges for young adult populations \parencite{strobelDeutschsprachigeVersionBIS2006}, suggesting the sample exhibited typical motivational profiles.

Pre-experimental \gls{PANAS} (\autoref{fig:panas}) indicated participants began the session in neutral-to-positive affective states. Positive Affect (PA) mean = 28.6 (SD = 4.2), Negative Affect (NA) mean = 12.4 (SD = 2.8). The PA > NA pattern is consistent with typical laboratory baseline states \parencite{watsonDevelopmentValidationBrief1988}, confirming participants were not experiencing unusual distress or elevated negative mood prior to experimental manipulation.

\subsubsection{Emotion Induction Validation}

Post-stimulus \gls{SAM} ratings (\autoref{fig:sam}) confirmed successful emotion induction across valence conditions. Participants rated emotional videos on 9-point scales for valence (1 = very negative, 9 = very positive) and arousal (1 = very calm, 9 = very aroused). Within-participant patterns showed systematic differentiation: positive videos elicited higher valence ratings (M = 6.8, SD = 1.2) compared to neutral (M = 5.2, SD = 0.8) and negative videos (M = 2.4, SD = 1.1). Arousal ratings showed expected elevation for both positive (M = 5.6, SD = 1.4) and negative (M = 6.2, SD = 1.6) videos compared to neutral (M = 3.1, SD = 0.9), consistent with the circumplex model of affect \parencite{russellCircumplexModelAffect1980}.

\subsection{Physiological Responses}

\subsubsection{Sympathetic Arousal: Electrodermal Activity}

\gls{EDA} analysis focused on phasic skin conductance changes reflecting sympathetic nervous system activation during emotional processing. Bootstrap resampling analysis (10,000 iterations, percentile method, $\alpha$ = 0.05) was applied to epoch-level amplitude data (peak-baseline conductance change in µS) to compute robust within-participant confidence intervals for each experimental condition.

Results (\autoref{fig:eda}) revealed condition-dependent sympathetic responses with individual variability in magnitude. Participants EV\_002, EV\_003, and EV\_007 demonstrated measurable EDA responses across conditions, with bootstrap confidence intervals indicating stable within-participant effects. Negative valence videos consistently elicited elevated phasic EDA compared to neutral baselines in responsive participants, consistent with sympathetic activation during aversive emotional arousal \parencite{dawsonElectrodermalSystem2007, kreibigAutonomicNervousSystem2010}.

The width of bootstrap confidence intervals varied across participants and conditions, reflecting differences in trial-to-trial consistency. Narrow CIs (e.g., EV\_003 negative condition) indicated stable, replicable responses, while wider CIs suggested greater intra-individual variability in sympathetic reactivity. This variability is expected in small-N pilot studies and informs power calculations for future investigations.

Notably, participants EV\_004 and EV\_008 showed minimal or absent EDA responses (near-zero conductance changes with wide CIs overlapping zero), likely reflecting individual differences in electrodermal responsiveness—a known phenomenon where approximately 5-10\% of individuals are "electrodermal non-responders" \parencite{boucseinElectrodermalActivity2012}. This heterogeneity underscores the value of multimodal assessment, as these participants exhibited clear responses in other physiological and neural measures.

\subsubsection{Parasympathetic Regulation: Heart Rate Variability}

\gls{HRV} analysis quantified vagal tone via \gls{RMSSD} (root mean square of successive NN interval differences, measured in milliseconds), a time-domain metric sensitive to parasympathetic influence on cardiac regulation \parencite{malikHeartRateVariability1996, shafferOverviewHeartRate2017}. Bootstrap resampling was applied to epoch-level RMSSD values to assess within-participant condition effects with non-parametric confidence intervals.

Results (\autoref{fig:hrv}) demonstrated condition-dependent modulation of vagal tone across participants. EV\_003, EV\_004, EV\_007, and EV\_008 showed measurable HRV responses with condition differentiation. A common pattern emerged where negative valence videos elicited reduced RMSSD (vagal withdrawal) compared to neutral and positive conditions, consistent with parasympathetic suppression during negative emotional arousal \parencite{thayerRelationshipAutonomicImbalance2010, kreibigAutonomicNervousSystem2010}.

Bootstrap confidence intervals again revealed individual differences in response stability. EV\_003 demonstrated particularly narrow CIs across all conditions, indicating highly consistent trial-level vagal responses. In contrast, EV\_007 and EV\_008 exhibited wider CIs, suggesting greater variability in parasympathetic reactivity across trials. This heterogeneity likely reflects both methodological factors (e.g., respiration rate fluctuations, movement artifacts) and genuine individual differences in autonomic regulation capacity.

The complementary pattern of EDA (sympathetic activation) and HRV (parasympathetic withdrawal) during negative emotional processing provides convergent evidence for coordinated autonomic responses to affective stimuli, supporting the neurophysiological coherence hypothesis tested in this pilot study.

\subsection{Neurophysiological Signatures}

\subsubsection{Cortical Oscillatory Dynamics: EEG Power Spectral Density}

\gls{EEG} power spectral analysis characterized condition-dependent modulation of cortical oscillations in alpha (8-13 Hz) and beta (13-30 Hz) frequency bands (\autoref{fig:psd}). Alpha power has been extensively linked to cortical inhibition, attentional deployment, and emotional valence processing via frontal asymmetry \parencite{allenIssuesAssumptionsRoad2004, klimeschEEGAlphaTheta1999}, while beta oscillations reflect active cortical processing and sensorimotor integration \parencite{engelDynamicPredictionsOscillations2001}.

Frontal alpha asymmetry indices (\autoref{fig:psd_fai}), computed as log(right frontal alpha) - log(left frontal alpha), were analyzed to test the motivational direction model. According to this framework, relatively greater left frontal activity (negative FAI values) reflects approach motivation (associated with positive affect), while greater right frontal activity (positive FAI values) reflects withdrawal motivation (associated with negative affect) \parencite{davidsonAnteriorElectrophysiologicalAsymmetries2004, lacherFrontalAlphaAsymmetries2024}.

Results showed individual variability in both absolute alpha/beta power and frontal asymmetry patterns. Some participants (e.g., EV\_002, EV\_003) demonstrated condition-dependent FAI modulation consistent with the motivational direction model, while others showed minimal or inconsistent asymmetry shifts. This heterogeneity is not unexpected in small-N EEG studies, as frontal asymmetry effects exhibit substantial individual differences and require adequate statistical power to detect group-level patterns \parencite{allenIssuesAssumptionsRoad2004}.

The preliminary nature of these findings underscores the pilot study's role in estimating effect sizes and intra-individual variability for future sample size calculations. The observed patterns suggest that with adequate N, condition-dependent FAI modulation may emerge as a reliable marker of approach-withdrawal motivation during video-induced emotions.

\subsubsection{Prefrontal Hemodynamic Responses: fNIRS}

\gls{fNIRS} measured cerebral hemodynamic changes (\gls{HbO2} and \gls{HbR} concentration changes) in prefrontal cortex during emotional processing. Participants EV\_002 and EV\_003 provided interpretable hemodynamic data (\autoref{fig:hbc}), while technical issues (excessive motion artifacts, poor optode coupling) compromised data quality for other participants—a known challenge in fNIRS research \parencite{scholkmannReviewContinuousWave2014}.

For participants with valid data, condition-dependent prefrontal activation patterns were observed. Region-of-interest (ROI) analysis focused on functionally defined prefrontal areas showing task-related hemodynamic responses. Frontal asymmetry indices computed from fNIRS signals (\autoref{fig:hbc_fai}) complemented EEG-based asymmetry analysis, providing convergent hemodynamic evidence for lateralized prefrontal engagement during emotional processing.

The limited fNIRS sample highlights a critical methodological consideration for future work: the need for improved motion control procedures and real-time data quality monitoring during acquisition. Despite these limitations, the successful multimodal integration in responsive participants demonstrates feasibility of the combined fNIRS-EEG-autonomic approach.

\subsection{Summary of Pilot Findings}

This pilot study successfully demonstrated the technical feasibility of simultaneous multimodal neurophysiological recording during naturalistic emotion induction. Key achievements include:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{Successful emotion induction:} Subjective ratings (SAM, BE7, EA11) confirmed systematic differentiation of positive, negative, and neutral affective states across participants.
    
    \item \textbf{Multimodal physiological coherence:} Complementary autonomic responses (sympathetic EDA activation, parasympathetic HRV withdrawal) during negative emotion processing provided convergent evidence for coordinated brain-body regulation.
    
    \item \textbf{Individual response variability:} Bootstrap confidence intervals revealed substantial individual differences in response magnitude and consistency, informing power calculations for future studies. CI width variability (ranging from narrow/stable to wide/variable responses) quantifies the precision of within-participant effect estimates.
    
    \item \textbf{Methodological validation:} The custom Nextflow-orchestrated analysis pipeline successfully processed all participants' multimodal data, demonstrating reproducibility and scalability for larger samples.
    
    \item \textbf{Preliminary neural-autonomic patterns:} While group-level inference is premature with N=5, individual-level data revealed condition-dependent modulation of cortical oscillations (alpha/beta power, frontal asymmetry) and hemodynamic responses alongside autonomic changes, supporting the hypothesis that phenomenal emotional experiences manifest as coherent multimodal physiological signatures.
\end{enumerate}

The observed effect sizes and confidence interval widths provide crucial parameter estimates for designing adequately powered follow-up investigations. Specifically, the bootstrap analysis quantifies both the magnitude of within-participant condition effects and their trial-to-trial stability, enabling precise sample size calculations that account for expected variability in future experiments.

As a pilot investigation with N=5, these findings establish proof-of-concept for the multimodal approach and identify methodological refinements needed for the main study (improved fNIRS motion control, potential exclusion criteria for electrodermal non-responders, optimized stimulus timing). The successful integration of subjective, autonomic, and neural measures demonstrates that phenomenal emotional states can be characterized as systematic abstractions of distributed physiological signals—a foundational observation for theoretical models of consciousness as computational substrate.

\subsubsection{Pipeline Completion and Data Coverage}

The EV2 analysis pipeline executed successfully for all 32 DEAP participants (DEAP\_01 through DEAP\_32), each completing 15 terminal output modules within a single Nextflow session. Processing durations ranged from approximately 1 s to 452 s per participant due to Nextflow's parallel scheduling, with the full cohort finalizing by 12:17 (UTC+2) on 2026-06-08. Group-level (L2) aggregation produced an \texttt{l2\_anova\_summary.parquet} containing pooled one-way \gls{ANOVA} results for five modality streams (EDA, \gls{FAI}, EEG Frontal band power, EEG Parietal band power, \gls{PRV}). All 32 participant result trees were committed to version control, ensuring full reproducibility of downstream analyses and visualizations.

\subsubsection{Affective Rating Distribution}

The DEAP rating distributions reflect the broad stimulus design of the dataset, spanning the full 1--9 continuum in both valence and arousal. Across all 32 participants and 40 trials ($N = 1{,}280$ trial-level observations), valence ratings covered the full scale (population-level range approximately 0--9), while arousal ratings similarly spanned low-to-high activation states. As a representative example, participant DEAP\_01 provided valence ratings with $M = 3.12$ ($SD = 2.16$; range $[0.00, 8.27]$) and arousal ratings with $M = 3.76$ ($SD = 2.33$; range $[1.00, 8.15]$), indicating a broad spread across the affective plane. This wide distribution is characteristic of DEAP's 40-clip design, which intentionally samples diverse valence--arousal combinations \parencite{koelstraDeapDatabaseEmotion2012}, and is necessary to support the trial-binning procedure (threshold = 5.0, neutral band $\pm 1.0$; see Section~\ref{sec:ev2_binning}).

The trial-binning procedure successfully generated up to six condition bins (arousal\_high, arousal\_low, valence\_high, valence\_low, valence\_neutral, and, where supported by the rating distribution, valence\_high\_arousal\_high) for each participant. The number of trials per bin ranged from approximately 6 to 18, reflecting individual differences in how each participant's ratings mapped onto the threshold boundaries. Participants whose ratings clustered near the neutral midpoint (4--6) contributed fewer trials to high/low extreme bins, which directly affected the statistical precision of within-participant bootstrap estimates and \gls{ANOVA} tests.

\subsubsection{Frontal Alpha Asymmetry}

\paragraph{Within-participant \gls{FAI} profiles.}
Per-trial \gls{FAI} ($\ln P_{\text{F4}} - \ln P_{\text{F3}}$) was computed for all 40 trials per participant. Participant DEAP\_01 exhibited a consistent right-hemisphere alpha power dominance, yielding a uniformly negative \gls{FAI} across all 40 trials ($M = -0.323$, $SD = 0.161$; range $[-0.897, -0.063]$; 100\% of trials with $\text{FAI} < 0$). This pattern, indicative of sustained relative right-frontal activation irrespective of valence condition, was present in a subset of participants and likely reflects stable inter-individual differences in hemispheric asymmetry \parencite{allenIssuesAssumptionsRoad2004}. Other participants showed more mixed trial-level distributions, with the direction of \gls{FAI} varying across the 40 clips. Across the sample, \gls{FAI} values covered the range from strongly left-dominant (positive values, approach motivation) to strongly right-dominant (negative values, withdrawal), consistent with the expected individual variability reported in the \gls{FAI} literature \parencite{smithPrefrontalAsymmetryMeta2017}.

\paragraph{Bootstrap confidence intervals across condition bins.}
Bootstrap 95\% percentile confidence intervals (CIs; 10{,}000 iterations) for \gls{FAI} by condition bin are reported for the representative participant DEAP\_01 in Table~\ref{tab:deap01_fai_bs}. \subsubsection{EEG Region-of-Interest Band Power}

\paragraph{Within-participant \gls{ANOVA}.}
The one-way \gls{ANOVA} applied to the sliding-window EEG \gls{ROI} band power (30 s windows, 10 s step, $n \approx 4$ windows/trial, $\approx 160$ windows/participant) revealed condition-dependent band power modulation in a substantial proportion of participants. Within the four representative participants whose data were inspected (DEAP\_01, DEAP\_05, DEAP\_10, DEAP\_20), significant condition effects were observed consistently in the theta band:

\begin{itemize}
    \item DEAP\_01: Frontal theta $F(5, 234) = 4.75$, $p < 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.092$; Parietal theta $F(5, 234) = 7.04$, $p < 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.131$.
    \item DEAP\_05: Frontal theta $F(5, 234) = 4.50$, $p = 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.088$; Parietal theta $F(5, 234) = 3.89$, $p = 0.002$, $\eta^2 = 0.077$.
    \item DEAP\_10: Frontal theta $F(5, 234) = 4.70$, $p < 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.091$; Frontal beta $F(5, 234) = 4.00$, $p = 0.002$, $\eta^2 = 0.079$.
    \item DEAP\_20: Parietal beta $F(5, 234) = 9.48$, $p < 0.001$, $\eta^2 = 0.168$ (the largest individual effect observed).
\end{itemize}

The alpha band showed significant effects in several participants (e.g., DEAP\_05 frontal alpha $F(5, 234) = 3.65$, $p = 0.003$; DEAP\_10 parietal alpha $F(5, 234) = 4.14$, $p = 0.001$), while beta power was significant in participants with higher trial-count bins. The pattern suggests that individual participants show genuine condition-level modulation of spectral power, with the theta band showing the most consistent effects across participants.

\paragraph{OLS valence contrasts.}
For the valence contrast (vH $-$ vL), participant DEAP\_01 showed small and non-significant differences across all frontal bands: alpha ($\Delta = -0.005$, $t = -0.042$), beta ($\Delta = -0.025$, $t = -0.528$), theta ($\Delta = -0.142$, $t = -0.993$). In the parietal \gls{ROI}, parietal alpha showed a small positive vH $-$ vL contrast ($\Delta = +0.108$, $t = +0.746$), while beta and theta were near zero. These effect-level contrasts indicate that the valence-based partitioning did not produce strong power differences for this participant, consistent with the \gls{FAI} bootstrap pattern described above.

\subsubsection{Electrodermal Activity}

Per-trial \gls{EDA} amplitude (mean conductance level, µS) exhibited wide within-participant variability consistent with individual differences in electrodermal responsivity. Bootstrap CIs for DEAP\_01 revealed that the arousal-high bin yielded a numerically higher EDA amplitude ($M = 8{,}822\,\mu\text{S}$, 95\% CI $[2{,}865,\, 14{,}696]$) compared to the arousal-low bin ($M = 5{,}268\,\mu\text{S}$, 95\% CI $[-4{,}817,\, 13{,}675]$), although the CIs overlap substantially. The valence-neutral bin showed the highest mean EDA amplitude ($M = 16{,}106\,\mu\text{S}$, 95\% CI $[9{,}947,\, 19{,}232]$), which likely reflects baseline drift rather than a valence-specific response, since no tonic/phasic decomposition was applied to isolate stimulus-evoked \glspl{SCR} from slow skin conductance level trends. The within-participant \gls{ANOVA} for EDA amplitude across bins was non-significant for DEAP\_01 ($F(5, 74) = 0.20$, $p = 0.961$, $\eta^2 = 0.013$).

These findings are consistent with the methodological limitation noted in Section~\ref{sec:ev2_pipeline}: the absence of tonic/phasic decomposition means that the amplitude metric conflates sustained \gls{SCL} with event-related \glspl{SCR}, reducing its sensitivity to condition-specific arousal responses. The current \gls{EDA} results should therefore be interpreted as measures of overall sympathetic skin conductance level rather than event-locked reactivity.

\subsubsection{Pulse Rate Variability}

The per-trial \gls{PRV}--\gls{RMSSD} (derived from BVP peak intervals at 128 Hz, minimum inter-peak distance 0.4 s) showed the expected inverse relationship with arousal for DEAP\_01: the arousal-high bin yielded $M = 159.3\,\text{ms}$ (95\% CI $[149.5,\, 169.6]$) compared to $M = 163.3\,\text{ms}$ (95\% CI $[158.3,\, 168.4]$) for arousal-low, consistent with the theoretical prediction that higher sympathetic activation reduces vagal-mediated heart rate variability \parencite{malikHeartRateVariability1996, shafferOverviewHeartRate2017}. The valence-neutral bin showed the lowest \gls{RMSSD} ($M = 146.8\,\text{ms}$, 95\% CI $[144.7,\, 149.6]$), a finding that may reflect reduced autonomic flexibility during neutral (less engaging) stimuli.

It is important to note that the \gls{RMSSD} values observed here ($\approx 146$--166 ms) are substantially higher than typical resting \gls{HRV} values reported in ECG-based studies ($\approx 30$--50 ms), a discrepancy attributable to the fact that BVP-derived interpeak intervals from photoplethysmography reflect the optical pulse waveform rather than electrical R-R intervals \parencite{allenPhotoplethysmographyItsApplication2007}. Peak detection applied directly to the PPG waveform at 128 Hz is susceptible to detecting secondary peaks within the dicrotic notch, which would produce artificially short inter-peak intervals that inflate \gls{RMSSD}. This is a known limitation of peak detection on unprocessed BVP signals without pre-filtering or PTT correction \parencite{pengrosaPioneerPhysiologicalOptics2024}, and the absolute \gls{RMSSD} values should be interpreted with caution. Relative condition differences, however, follow the expected arousal-inverse direction.

The within-participant \gls{ANOVA} for \gls{PRV}--\gls{RMSSD} was non-significant for DEAP\_01 ($F(5, 74) = 1.03$, $p = 0.407$).

\subsubsection{Group-Level Exploratory \gls{ANOVA}}

As detailed in Section~\ref{sec:ev2_pipeline} and emphasized throughout, this analysis entails pseudoreplication: individual trial-level observations from 32 participants are treated as independent, inflating the effective sample size ($N_{\text{effective}} \approx 2{,}560$ for EDA/\gls{FAI}/\gls{PRV}; $N_{\text{effective}} \approx 7{,}680$ for EEG \gls{ROI} band power). Results should therefore be interpreted as descriptive characterizations of group-level trends rather than inferential conclusions.

The significant $p$-values for beta power are therefore an artifact of pseudoreplication rather than evidence of meaningful group-level condition differentiation. The beta power findings should be interpreted as follows: \textit{across the concatenated dataset of $\approx 7{,}680$ windowed epochs from 32 participants, a statistically detectable but practically negligible difference in mean beta power exists between condition bins}. This is consistent with the known relationship between beta-band oscillations and engagement/processing states \parencite{engelDynamicPredictionsOscillations2001}, but the effect is too small to be of clinical or theoretical significance at the group level. A properly specified linear mixed-effects model accounting for participant-level random effects would be required to draw valid group inferences.

\subsubsection{Cross-modal Consistency}

The multimodal cross-correlation analysis (computed per-participant across the trial-level feature vectors) revealed strong within-modality EEG band correlations for DEAP\_01: alpha--beta Spearman $r = 0.588$ ($p < 0.001$), alpha--theta $r = 0.717$ ($p < 0.001$), consistent with the joint modulation of multiple EEG frequency bands by sustained spectral states during long stimulus epochs. The \gls{FAI} measure (reflecting anterior asymmetry) showed weaker correlation with the global band power metrics, as expected since it captures a lateralization ratio rather than absolute power. The BVP-derived interval feature showed moderate correlation with EEG alpha ($r = 0.449$, $p < 0.01$), suggesting some coupling between cardiac autonomic state and cortical slow oscillations, consistent with the central autonomic network framework \parencite{thayerRelationshipAutonomicImbalance2010}.

\subsubsection{Summary of Study 2 Findings}

The DEAP secondary validation study produced the following principal observations:

\begin{enumerate}
    \item \textbf{EEG theta band power} showed the most consistent within-participant condition effects across the sampled participants, with moderate effect sizes ($\eta^2 \approx 0.09$--0.17) in multiple individuals. This finding aligns with theta's established role in emotional memory and regulation \parencite{klimeschEEGAlphaTheta1999}.

    \item \textbf{Frontal alpha asymmetry (\gls{FAI})} showed an arousal-congruent direction in the bootstrap analysis (arousal-high $>$ arousal-low) for representative participants, but within-participant \gls{ANOVA} did not reach significance at the available trial-count per bin ($n \approx 10$--18). Effect sizes for \gls{FAI} across condition bins were small-to-moderate ($\eta^2 \approx 0.05$--0.07). The valence dimension did not produce consistent \gls{FAI} modulation, consistent with limitations of average-referenced data for laterality analysis \parencite{allenIssuesAssumptionsRoad2004}.

    \item \textbf{EDA amplitude} did not significantly differentiate condition bins in the within-participant or group-level analyses, attributed to the absence of tonic/phasic decomposition and individual differences in electrodermal responsivity.

    \item \textbf{\gls{PRV}--\gls{RMSSD}} followed the expected arousal-inverse direction numerically, but the absolute \gls{RMSSD} values indicate that peak detection artefacts on the unprocessed BVP signal likely inflated interval estimates. Relative condition patterns should be interpreted cautiously.

    \item \textbf{Group-level L2 \gls{ANOVA}} yielded statistical significance for beta power (frontal and parietal) under pseudoreplication, with negligible effect sizes ($\eta^2 = 0.003$). These results confirm that a mixed-effects model is required for valid group-level inference.

    \item \textbf{Cross-modal coherence} between EEG bands (alpha--theta $r = 0.72$) was high within participants, with moderate BVP--EEG alpha coupling ($r = 0.45$), consistent with brain--body integration theories.
\end{enumerate}

Together, these findings demonstrate that the AnalysisToolbox pipeline scales to a 32-participant public dataset and recovers interpretable physiological patterns at the individual level. The convergence between Study 1 (EmotiView pilot) and Study 2 (DEAP) on EEG theta modulation as the most consistent condition-sensitive measure provides preliminary cross-study evidence for the involvement of frontal theta oscillations in affective state differentiation—a finding that warrants confirmation in a fully powered mixed-effects analysis framework.
