Emotional states are fundamentally embodied, emerging from the dynamic interplay between central neural processing and peripheral physiological adjustments orchestrated by the autonomic nervous system (ANS). While ANS outputs such as heart rate variability (HRV) and electrodermal activity (EDA) reflect emotional arousal and valence, understanding how brain activity and these peripheral signals co-vary across emotional conditions is crucial for elucidating brain-body interactions. This pilot study investigates the consistency of neural and autonomic response profiles during the conscious processing of distinct emotional states (positive, negative, neutral) in five participants using a within-participant, multimodal approach.

We simultaneously recorded high-temporal-resolution electroencephalography (EEG), electrocardiography (ECG) for HRV analysis (specifically Root Mean Square of Successive Differences, RMSSD), EDA, and functional near-infrared spectroscopy (fNIRS) while participants viewed validated emotional video clips from the E-MOVIE database. Because the reduced sample size precluded the group-level phase-synchrony analysis originally envisioned, we adopted a within-participant analytical strategy combining sliding-window feature extraction (30-second windows, 10-second steps), bootstrap resampling (10,000 iterations with percentile confidence intervals), and ordinary least squares regression to estimate condition-specific effects for each modality. Cross-modal condition profiling then assessed whether the pattern of emotional modulation was consistent across EEG spectral power, HRV (RMSSD), and EDA metrics within each individual.

We hypothesized that emotional conditions would produce distinct within-participant physiological profiles reported as reliable bootstrap confidence intervals, and that the direction of condition-specific effects would show cross-modal consistency---indicating coordinated brain-body responses to emotional content. We additionally examined frontal alpha asymmetry as a marker of hemispheric motivational direction and assessed fNIRS-derived hemispheric differences in prefrontal hemodynamic activity. By characterising within-participant condition profiles and their cross-modal coherence, this pilot study provides initial evidence for the feasibility of an individual-level, multimodal approach to studying embodied emotional processing. The fully automated Nextflow analysis pipeline developed for this study ensures complete reproducibility and direct scalability to larger samples in future confirmatory investigations, where group-level synchrony analyses will become viable.
